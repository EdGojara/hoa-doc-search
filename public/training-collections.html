Collecting Delinquent Assessments

Dignity Through the Entire Process.
The Bedrock Promise

The Bedrock Promise

Our Creed

At Bedrock, we treat every homeowner, board member, vendor, and coworker with dignity and respect. We enforce community standards fairly and consistently. We solve problems, communicate clearly, and continuously improve our systems.

This applies to collections too — even when the conversation is uncomfortable.

Foundation

The Most Important Reframe

We are not collecting money for Bedrock. We are collecting money for the community.

Every unpaid assessment is a cost that other homeowners absorb.

The pool doesn't get maintained. The landscaping gets cut. The reserves get depleted.

When we collect assessments, we are protecting every homeowner who does pay — on time, every month, without complaint.

That framing changes everything about how you approach a collections conversation.

Principle

Separate the Person from the Debt

The homeowner may be facing circumstances you know nothing about.

Job loss. Medical bills. Divorce. Death in the family.

That doesn't eliminate the debt.

It does change how you talk about it.

Think:

"This homeowner may be going through something difficult."

And simultaneously:

"The Association has an obligation to collect assessments fairly and consistently."

Both are true at the same time.

Hold both.

Diagnostic

Why Collections Get Difficult

SituationWhat's Actually Happening

Homeowner disputes the balanceConfusion. Prior errors. Lack of records.
Homeowner claims hardshipMay be genuine. May be a delay tactic.
Homeowner is angryEmbarrassment coming out as aggression.

Understanding the situation changes your approach.

A homeowner who is ashamed needs a different conversation than one who is strategic.

The Process

The Bedrock Collections Process

1
Reminder notice

Friendly, clear, specific balance and due date.

2
Late fee applied

Automatic, consistent, no exceptions without board approval.

3
Formal notice

Certified mail, specific language per Texas Property Code.

4
Payment plan opportunity

Offered before escalation, documented in trustEd.

5
Attorney referral

When all other options are exhausted.

6
Legal action

Only when necessary, always a last resort.

Your Authority

You are responsible for steps 1 through 4.

Steps 5 and 6 go to Ed.

Never skip steps. Never jump to threats of legal action before the process is followed. Never promise outcomes you cannot guarantee.

Conversation

The Payment Plan Conversation

"I understand you're in a difficult spot right now. The Association does offer payment plans for homeowners who are working to get current. If that's something that would help, I can put together a proposal for Ed to review. What I need from you is a commitment to the plan and to stay current on your ongoing assessments while we work through the balance."

Payment plans require Ed's approval.

Document everything in trustEd.

If they miss a payment on the plan, escalate immediately.

The Framework

The Collections Framework

1
Establish contact

"I'm reaching out because there's a balance on your account and I want to help you get this resolved before it moves further in the process."

2
Understand the situation

"Can you help me understand what's going on? I want to make sure we're working with the full picture."

3
Clarify the balance

"Let me walk you through exactly what makes up this balance so we're both looking at the same numbers."

4
Present options
  • Pay in full
  • Payment plan (subject to approval)
  • Dispute a specific charge (document and escalate)
5
Set a clear next step

"So what I'm hearing is you can make a payment of $X by Friday. I'll note that in your account. Can I follow up with you Monday to confirm it went through?"

Language

What To Never Say

Never Say
  • "If you don't pay we'll put a lien on your house." (Before the proper process)
  • "The attorney is going to contact you." (Before the referral is actually made)
  • "Everyone else pays on time."
  • "I don't know why you haven't paid."
  • "This isn't my problem."
Response

When They Dispute the Balance

"I want to make sure we have this right. Let me pull up your account history and walk through it with you. If there's an error, we'll correct it."

Then actually check.

If there is an error, own it immediately and correct it.

If the balance is correct, walk them through it line by line until they understand.

Response

When They Claim Hardship

Take hardship claims at face value unless you have clear evidence otherwise.

"I hear you and I want to help find a path forward. Let me bring this to our management team and see what options we can put together. Can I follow up with you by Wednesday with a proposal?"

Then follow up by Wednesday.

Statutory Reality

The Texas Property Code Reality

Texas Property Code Chapter 209 governs HOA collections.

There are specific notice requirements, timelines, and procedures that must be followed before a lien can be filed or legal action can be taken.

What you need to know

You do not need to know every detail of Chapter 209.

You do need to know this: follow the process, document everything, and escalate before taking any action outside your authority.

If you're ever unsure whether an action is permitted, ask Ed before you act.

Role Play

Scenario 4A — The Avoider

Role Play Scenario 4A
The Avoider
Setup
A homeowner is three months behind on assessments. You've sent two notices with no response. You reach them by phone.
Yeah I know I owe money. I've just been dealing with some stuff. I'll get to it.
✗ Wrong Response
"Your account is 90 days past due and if we don't receive payment by Friday we'll be referring this to our attorney."
Why it fails
Jumped to threats. No understanding of the situation. Pushes them back into avoidance.
✓ Right Response
"I appreciate you taking my call. I'm not calling to pressure you — I'm calling because I want to help you get this resolved before it moves further in the process. Can you tell me a little about what's been going on? I want to make sure we're working with the right options for your situation."
Why it works
Took the pressure off immediately. Opened a real conversation. Positioned Bedrock as trying to help not trying to collect. Gathered information before presenting options.
Debrief Questions
  • What if they say they just don't have the money right now?
  • When do you offer a payment plan versus escalate?
  • What do you document after this call?
Role Play

Scenario 4B — The Dispute

Role Play Scenario 4B
The Dispute
Setup
A homeowner calls saying their balance is wrong and they're not paying until it's corrected.
I've been charged late fees I shouldn't have. I paid on time and nobody applied it correctly. I'm not paying a dime until this is fixed.
✗ Wrong Response
"Our records show the payment was received late. The late fee stands."
Why it fails
Defensive. Didn't verify. Creates a fight.
✓ Right Response
"I want to make sure we have this right — if there's an error, I want to find it and fix it. Can you tell me when you made the payment and how? I'll pull up the account and we'll go through it together line by line."
Why it works
Took the dispute seriously. Committed to verifying before defending. Positioned as solving together not arguing. Created a path to resolution.
Debrief Questions
  • What if after review the late fee is correct?
  • How do you communicate a correction if we made an error?
  • What if they paid by check and say it cleared their bank but wasn't applied?
Role Play

Scenario 4C — The Angry Non-Payer

Role Play Scenario 4C
The Angry Non-Payer
Setup
A homeowner is six months behind. They call angry before you reach out to them.
Don't send me another letter. I know I owe money. The HOA wastes money on things we don't need and I'm not paying for it.
✗ Wrong Response
"Your assessments are required by your governing documents regardless of how you feel about how the money is spent."
Why it fails
Technically correct. Completely ineffective. Escalates the anger.
✓ Right Response
"I hear you — and I understand that frustration with how assessments are spent is real for a lot of homeowners. I want to address two things separately if that's okay. The first is your concerns about how the community's money is being managed — that's something the board needs to hear and I can tell you how to raise that formally. The second is the balance on your account, because regardless of those concerns, the Association does depend on assessments to operate and other homeowners are affected when accounts are past due. Can we talk about both?"
Why it works
Validated their concern without agreeing. Separated the governance issue from the collections issue. Gave them a legitimate path for their complaint. Kept the collections conversation on track.
Debrief Questions
  • What if they refuse to separate the two issues?
  • How do you escalate a homeowner who raises legitimate governance concerns?
  • At what point does this go to the attorney regardless of the conversation?

Reference

The Five Bedrock Values — Applied to Collections

No. 01
Hospitality
Treat every homeowner in collections with dignity. They may be going through something difficult. How we treat them in their worst moment defines our character.
No. 02
Ownership
Own every delinquent account through the process. Don't send a notice and forget it. Follow the sequence and follow up.
No. 03
Consistency
Apply the same process to every delinquent account. No exceptions without board approval. Selective collection creates legal exposure.
No. 04
Communication
Keep homeowners informed at every step. No surprises. No sudden attorney referrals without proper notice.
No. 05
Improvement
When collections problems cluster, ask why. Is there a billing clarity issue? A payment portal problem? A community economic trend the board needs to know about?

Practice

The Weekly Practice

Every Monday for the first month, five minutes:

One collections scenario. Rotate who plays the homeowner.

After each role play:

  • Did we separate the person from the debt?
  • Did we understand the situation before presenting options?
  • Did we set a specific next step?
  • What's the one thing to remember?
The One Thing — If you remember nothing else
We collect assessments because communities depend on them.
We do it with dignity because people deserve it.
Both are true.
Neither cancels the other.
Hold both. Every single time.
