Getting People to Comply with Rules

Consistency Builds Trust.
The Bedrock Promise

The Bedrock Promise

Our Creed

At Bedrock, we treat every homeowner, board member, vendor, and coworker with dignity and respect. We enforce community standards fairly and consistently. We solve problems, communicate clearly, and continuously improve our systems.

Enforcement is not punishment.

Enforcement is how we protect the community everyone chose to live in.

Foundation

The Most Important Reframe

We are not trying to win arguments. We are trying to achieve compliance.

The goal is never: "I proved I was right."

The goal is always: "The violation was corrected."

Those are completely different objectives and they require completely different approaches.

Diagnostic

Why People Resist Rules

What They SayWhat They're Really Asking
"Why me?"Are you treating everyone the same?
"I've done this for years."Why is this suddenly a problem?
"My neighbor does the same thing."Are you being consistent?
"This rule is ridiculous."Can you help me understand why it exists?
"I didn't know."Why wasn't I told sooner?

Almost every compliance resistance comes down to one of two things:

Fairness — They want to know the rule applies to everyone equally.

Understanding — They want to know why the rule exists.

Give them both and resistance drops dramatically.

Pattern Recognition

The Violations People Fight Hardest

  • Parking — Personal and immediate. Their car, their property, their convenience.
  • Landscaping — Subjective. What looks maintained to them may not meet standards.
  • Trash cans — Feels petty. Hard to understand why it matters.
  • Architectural modifications — They spent money. Emotional and financial investment.
  • Commercial vehicles and trailers — Livelihood. Their work truck feels personal.

For each of these, the approach is the same:

Explain the rule. Explain why it exists. Explain how it protects the community. Tell them what needs to happen next.

The Framework

The Compliance Framework

1
Lead with why, not what

Don't start with the violation. Start with the purpose of the standard.

Bad: "Your trash cans need to be stored out of sight per Section 6.2."

Better: "The community adopted a standard for trash can storage because visible cans affect how the neighborhood looks and impacts property values for everyone. I want to help you get this resolved quickly."

2
Acknowledge their perspective

"I understand this may feel like a small thing."

"I can see you weren't aware this was an issue."

You're not agreeing the rule is wrong.

You're acknowledging their experience of it.

3
Be specific about what's needed

Bad: "You'll need to address this."

Better: "Specifically, the trash cans need to be stored inside the garage or behind a fence screen when not on the curb for collection. That's what we need to see by [specific date]."

4
Give them a path and a timeline

"Here's what needs to happen and here's the timeline."

"If you have questions about what meets the standard, I'm happy to help you figure that out."

5
Document and follow up

Set a follow-up date in trustEd.

If the violation isn't resolved by the deadline, follow the enforcement sequence.

Don't let it sit. Inconsistent follow-up is what creates the "Why me?" problem.

Principle

The Consistency Principle in Action

When a homeowner says "Why me?" the honest answer must be:

"Because this standard applies to everyone and we enforce it consistently."

If you cannot say that honestly, there is a process problem that needs to be escalated.

Selective enforcement is not just unfair.

In Texas HOA law, it creates legal exposure for the Association.

If you are ever asked to enforce a rule against one homeowner while ignoring the same violation at another property, escalate to Ed immediately.

Response

The "I Didn't Know" Response

Don't argue about whether they should have known.

Don't quote the section of the governing documents they should have read.

Instead:

"I understand — these documents can be hard to navigate. Let me make sure you have what you need going forward. Here's what the standard requires and here's what we need to see resolved. I want to make this as easy as possible for you."

Then make a note in trustEd. If multiple homeowners are saying they didn't know about the same rule, that's an education gap we need to fix.

Response

The "My Neighbor Does It Too" Response

Never say: "I can't discuss other properties."

That sounds evasive and damages trust.

Instead say:

"I hear you — consistency matters to us too. I can tell you that we apply these standards community-wide. If there's a specific property you're concerned about, you can submit a complaint through the portal and it will be reviewed the same way yours was."

Then follow through. If the neighbor is actually in violation, address it.

Nothing destroys trust faster than enforcing rules selectively.

Crisis Protocol

When They Refuse to Comply

Some homeowners will refuse regardless of how well you handle the interaction.

That's okay. Your job is not to make them happy about the rule.

Your job is to enforce it consistently and professionally.

When they refuse:

  • Document the interaction in trustEd
  • Confirm the violation in writing
  • Move to the next step in the enforcement sequence
  • Escalate to Ed if you're unsure about the next step

Never make threats you're not authorized to make.

Never promise outcomes you can't guarantee.

Never skip steps in the enforcement sequence.

Language

What To Never Say

Never Say
  • "The board told me to send this."
  • "I'm just doing my job."
  • "You signed the CC&Rs when you bought the house."
  • "Other homeowners don't complain about this."
  • "If you don't fix it we'll fine you." (Before the proper notice sequence)
Role Play

Scenario 3A — The Parking Violation

Role Play Scenario 3A
The Parking Violation
Setup
A homeowner calls upset about a notice for parking a commercial van in the driveway overnight.
This is my work van. I need it for my job. Where am I supposed to park it? I've been parking here for two years.
✗ Wrong Response
"Section 8 of the CC&Rs prohibits commercial vehicles in driveways. You need to find alternative parking."
Why it fails
No acknowledgment. No explanation of why. No path forward. Feels punitive.
✓ Right Response
"I completely understand — your work van is how you make a living and this matters to you. Let me explain what the community standard is and why it exists, and then let's figure out what options you have. The community adopted this standard because commercial vehicles can affect the neighborhood's appearance and property values. What I need to understand is what the van looks like — does it have commercial lettering or markings on it? The answer may affect what options are available to you."
Why it works
Acknowledged the livelihood concern. Explained the why behind the rule. Asked a clarifying question before making a judgment. Opened the door to finding a solution.
Debrief Questions
  • What if the van has no commercial markings — does that change anything?
  • What if there's no alternative parking available to them?
  • How do you follow up if they say they'll think about it?
Role Play

Scenario 3B — The Landscaping Notice

Role Play Scenario 3B
The Landscaping Notice
Setup
A homeowner calls angry about a violation notice for dead grass. They say they've been watering it and it's not dead, just dormant.
This is insane. My grass is not dead, it's dormant. It's summer in Texas. Do you know how expensive water is? I'm not going to overwater my lawn just because someone thinks it looks brown.
✗ Wrong Response
"The standard requires maintained landscaping. Dormant grass that appears dead is a violation."
Why it fails
Argumentative. Dismissive. Creates a fight about facts.
✓ Right Response
"That's actually a fair point and I want to make sure we're applying this correctly. Dormant grass and dead grass can look similar and we want to make sure we're not citing you unfairly. Can you tell me what the grass looks like specifically — is it brown throughout or patchy? And what's your current watering schedule? I want to take a second look at this before we go any further."
Why it works
Took their point seriously. Didn't double down on the violation before verifying. Asked questions to get the facts. Showed willingness to review rather than just enforce.
Debrief Questions
  • What if after review the grass is actually dead?
  • How do you communicate a correction if we cited incorrectly?
  • When do you escalate a dispute about whether something is actually a violation?
Role Play

Scenario 3C — The Repeat Violator

Role Play Scenario 3C
The Repeat Violator
Setup
A homeowner has received three notices for the same violation — trash cans left at the curb. They call dismissive and argumentative.
I got another letter. I don't have time to deal with this. Just send me the fine and leave me alone.
✗ Wrong Response
"We will be issuing a fine per the enforcement schedule."
Why it fails
Transactional. Misses an opportunity to actually achieve compliance.
✓ Right Response
"I hear you — I know getting repeated letters on the same thing is frustrating. I want to understand what's making this difficult so we can actually solve it. Is it a timing issue with when collection happens versus when you're able to bring the cans in? Sometimes there's a simple fix that makes this easier for everyone."
Why it works
Acknowledged their frustration without backing down. Looked for the root cause instead of just enforcing. Showed genuine interest in solving the problem. Still moves toward compliance without being punitive.
Debrief Questions
  • What if they genuinely don't care and just want the fine?
  • At what point does this escalate to the attorney?
  • How do you document a pattern of repeat violations?

Reference

The Five Bedrock Values — Applied to Compliance

No. 01
Hospitality
Enforce the rule with warmth. Explain why it exists. Treat the homeowner as a guest who deserves to understand the standards of the community they live in.
No. 02
Ownership
Own the enforcement process from notice to resolution. Don't send a letter and forget it. Follow up.
No. 03
Consistency
Apply every standard the same way to every homeowner. Every time. That's what fairness actually looks like.
No. 04
Communication
Tell homeowners exactly what's needed, by when, and what happens next. No ambiguity. No surprises.
No. 05
Improvement
When the same violation keeps coming up, ask why. Is the rule unclear? Is the notice confusing? Is there an education gap we could close proactively?

Practice

The Weekly Practice

Every Monday for the first month, five minutes:

One compliance scenario. Rotate who plays the homeowner.

After each role play:

  • Did we lead with why or with what?
  • Did we acknowledge their perspective?
  • Did we give a specific path forward?
  • What's the one thing to remember?
The One Thing — If you remember nothing else
Consistency builds trust.
Every homeowner who feels they were treated fairly becomes a community asset.
Every homeowner who feels they were treated unfairly becomes a community problem.
Enforce the standard.
Explain the why.
Apply it consistently.
Every single time.
