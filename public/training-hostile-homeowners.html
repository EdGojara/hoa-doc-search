Hostile and Abusive Homeowners

Enforce Standards with Hospitality.
The Bedrock Promise

The Bedrock Promise

Our Creed

At Bedrock, we treat every homeowner, board member, vendor, and coworker with dignity and respect. We enforce community standards fairly and consistently. We solve problems, communicate clearly, and continuously improve our systems.

This is not a customer service script.

This is who we are.

Foundation

The Most Important Distinction

Every person deserves dignity. Not every request can be granted.

We are not a "customer is always right" company.

We are a "every person deserves to be treated with respect" company.

Those are completely different things.

Our Motto

The Cultural Motto

The Motto
Enforce Standards with Hospitality.

Most HOA companies do one or the other. They either enforce harshly or accommodate everything.

We do both at the same time.

That is hard. That is the standard.

Boundaries

Protecting Our Employees

Hospitality does not require accepting abuse.

We treat every person with dignity and respect. We expect the same in return.

Your Authority

You are empowered to set boundaries calmly and professionally.

Being kind does not mean being a punching bag.

Bedrock supports you when you hold that line.

Mission

We Are Not in the Business of Sending Violation Letters

We are in the business of improving communities.

Violation letters are a tool. They are not the mission.

When enforcement is required, we use it. But we never forget that behind every letter is a person who lives in a community we are responsible for improving.

Locus of Control

The Three Things We Control

We cannot control:

  • Board decisions
  • Governing documents
  • Homeowner behavior
  • Whether someone agrees with the rules

We can control:

  • Our professionalism
  • Our communication
  • Our follow-through

When someone is demanding the impossible, come back to what you can control.

The Framework

The Four-Step Framework

1
Understand

Before anything else, understand what actually happened from their perspective.

"Help me understand what happened from your perspective."

"Walk me through what you received and what your concern is."

You are gathering information, not conceding anything.

Understanding is required. Agreement is not.

2
Acknowledge

Before any policy, before any explanation, acknowledge what they said.

"I understand why receiving that letter would be frustrating."

"I can see why this feels unfair from where you're standing."

You are not agreeing with them.

You are confirming that you heard them.

3
Educate First, Enforce When Necessary

Explain not just the rule but why it exists.

Bad: "Section 4.3 of the CC&Rs states that..."

Better: "The community adopted this standard because..."

You are an educator first.

You are an enforcer when necessary.

4
Resolve

Give them a clear path forward.

"Here's what I can do from here."

"Here's what the process looks like and what to expect."

Never leave an interaction without a next step.

Language

What To Never Say

Never Say
  • "That's the policy."
  • "There's nothing I can do."
  • "That's not my department."
  • "You should have read your governing documents."
  • "Other homeowners don't have this problem."
  • "That's what the board wants."
Instead Say
  • "The Association's governing documents and policies require..."
  • "The board has adopted this standard for the community."
Decision Tool

The Bedrock Decision Filter

When you're unsure what to do, ask four questions:

1Does this treat people with dignity?
2Does this protect the community?
3Does this apply standards consistently?
4Does this improve trust?

If yes to all four, you're making the right decision.

Self-Regulation

The Pause Principle

When emotions rise, slow down.

We do not make important decisions when people are angry — including ourselves.

If you feel yourself getting reactive, pause.

Take a breath.

Come back to what you can control.

Principle

The Fairness Principle

Consistency builds trust.

Many homeowners don't object to the rules. They object to inconsistent application of the rules.

When a homeowner says "Why me?" they are almost always really asking "Are you treating everyone the same way?"

If you are ever unsure whether an action is consistent with how similar situations have been handled, ask before you act.

Discipline

Ownership — What It Actually Means

If you receive it, you own communication until it reaches resolution or is formally transferred.

Ownership means:

  • You never drop the ball
  • You always know the status
  • You always communicate the next step

Sometimes ownership means escalating, delegating, or coordinating.

It never means dropping the ball.

Standard

The 24-Hour Rule

Every communication receives a response within one business day.

Even if the answer is simply:

"We've received your message and are working on it. I'll update you by [specific day]."

People tolerate delays.

They cannot tolerate uncertainty.

Service Discipline

The Last 5%

Most companies solve problems. Few companies close loops.

The last 5% of service creates 95% of loyalty.

Examples:

  • Calling when you promised to call
  • Sending the follow-up after resolution
  • Checking back after a problem is solved
  • Telling a homeowner "We made an improvement because of your feedback"
Crisis Protocol

When It Escalates

If a homeowner becomes verbally abusive say:

"Mr. Smith, I want to help work through this with you. To do that, I need us to be able to have a conversation together. Can we do that?"

If it continues:

"I understand you're very frustrated. Let me give you some time, and I'll follow up with you personally tomorrow morning at 9am."

Then document everything in trustEd immediately.

Never:

  • Match their tone
  • Threaten consequences in anger
  • Leave without a concrete next step
  • Fail to follow up when you said you would

Role Play

Scenario 1A — The Violation Letter

Role Play Scenario 1A
The Violation Letter
Setup
A homeowner calls furious about a violation notice for an unpainted fence. Fifteen years, never had a problem.
This is absolutely ridiculous. I've lived here fifteen years and nobody has ever said anything. Now I get this letter? I want to know who complained and I want this dropped immediately.
✗ Wrong Response
"Sir, Section 7 of the CC&Rs requires all fences to be painted. The violation stands."
Why it fails
Led with policy. No acknowledgment. Creates an adversary.
✓ Right Response
"I completely understand — fifteen years and hearing from us out of the blue about your fence would catch anyone off guard. Can you tell me a little more about what the letter said and what the fence looks like currently? I want to make sure I have the full picture before we talk about next steps."
Why it works
Acknowledged their history. Asked a question. Gathered information. Kept the relationship intact.
Debrief Questions
  • What do you say if they demand to know who complained?
  • How do you explain the rule without it sounding punitive?
  • What's the next step if the violation is real and valid?
Role Play

Scenario 1B — The ARC Denial

Role Play Scenario 1B
The ARC Denial
Setup
Homeowner calls angry that their pergola application was denied after $500 in professional drawings.
I cannot believe this. I submitted everything, paid for drawings, and now you're telling me no? What is the point of this process?
✗ Wrong Response
"The board reviewed your application and determined it doesn't meet community standards."
Why it fails
Hides behind the board. No acknowledgment. No path forward.
✓ Right Response
"I'm really sorry this didn't go the way you hoped, especially after investing in professional drawings. Can I pull up your application and walk through exactly what the board's concerns were? Sometimes there are modifications that could get this approved, and I want to make sure you have every option available to you."
Why it works
Acknowledged the investment. Offered a path forward. Kept the door open.
Debrief Questions
  • What if the decision is truly final?
  • How do you say no in a way that doesn't feel dismissive?
  • What's the difference between defending the board and explaining the board's reasoning?
Role Play

Scenario 1C — The Abusive Caller

Role Play Scenario 1C
The Abusive Caller
Setup
Homeowner calls screaming about a fine. Cursing. Threatening to sue.
You people are crooks. I'm going to sue every single one of you. I'm not paying a damn thing.
✗ Wrong Response
"Sir, I'm going to have to end this call if you continue speaking to me that way."
Why it fails
Creates a power struggle. Escalates. Backs you into a corner.
✓ Right Response
"Mr. Smith, I want to help work through this with you. To do that, I need us to be able to have a conversation together. Can we do that?"
If it continues: "I understand. Let me give you some time. I'll follow up with you personally tomorrow morning at 9am so we can go through this step by step."
Why it works
Stayed calm. Kept the door open. Took ownership of the next step. Gave a specific time.
Debrief Questions
  • What do you document after a call like this?
  • What happens at 9am if they don't answer?
  • What if they threaten legal action? Who do you notify?
