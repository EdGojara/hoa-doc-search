This packet covers everything your team needs to file architectural submissions cleanly: the submission portal, the review process, our service-level commitments, the master plan library that fast-tracks repeat plans, fees, and points of contact. We've kept it short on purpose. Builders move fast, and the system is built so we can too.

1. The submission portal

All architectural submissions for this community flow through a dedicated builder portal. Each submission is structured (lot, plan, elevation, materials, attachments), receives a unique reference number on intake, and tracks status in real time so you don't have to email asking "did you get my submission?"

Submissions land in our review queue automatically. The reviewing manager works through them in order received. As your team grows beyond one coordinator, additional coordinators at your company can be added to the portal with email-based access — just send the list to the contact below.

2. How submissions are reviewed

Every submission is reviewed against three layers:

  • Design Guidelines for this community — masonry coverage, exterior materials, fence specifications, height restrictions, setbacks, and elevation standards
  • Site-specific conditions applicable to the particular lot — corner-lot rules, common-area-facing requirements, easement constraints, thoroughfare setbacks
  • Master plan match — if the proposed plan and elevation are already in the master library for this community, the submission fast-tracks (see Section 4)

The reviewer issues one of four decisions on each submission:

  • Approved. The submission proceeds to construction as proposed.
  • Approved with conditions. The submission may proceed subject to the listed conditions (e.g., color substitution, fence material constraint). The conditions appear in the approval letter and bind the construction.
  • Denied. Specific items require revision. The denial letter lists the items so a revised submission can be filed.
  • Additional information requested. The reviewer asks for clarifying information before the decision clock continues.

4. The master plan library

Your team likely builds a finite catalog of plans across each elevation — perhaps eight to twelve plans, each available in three to four elevations. The master plan library exists to compress review time for this structural repetition.

The first submission of each plan-and-elevation combination at this community runs the standard 5-business-day review. Once approved, the plan goes into the master library for this community with its full material specification preserved. Every subsequent submission of the same plan and elevation — for a different lot — auto-matches the master, fast-tracks to 2 business days, and is reviewed only for site-specific items (orientation, neighbor adjacency, lot-type conditions).

Practical implication
At 100 submissions per year with twelve approved master plans, roughly 88 of those submissions fast-track. That's the difference between an architectural-review process that bottlenecks construction starts and one that doesn't. The master library scales — every approval makes the next one faster.

To populate the library quickly: your team can submit each elevation-and-plan combination once as soon as the offering is finalized, well in advance of breaking ground. We review them on the standard SLA. Once approved, the library is ready for the lot-by-lot submissions that follow.

5. What to attach with each submission

Required

  • Site plan — plat with footprint, driveway, and setbacks
  • Front elevation
  • Rear elevation
  • Floor plan — all floors if multi-story

Recommended

  • Left and right side elevations — required if the lot is corner, common-area-facing, or has any side-yard adjacency conditions
  • Color board or material specification sheet — a photo of the physical color board or a vendor spec sheet

The portal captures the full material specification in structured fields — brick color, stone, masonry coverage percentages, siding, trim, shutters, doors, roof, fence material. The approval letter reprints those exact specifications, so submitting accurate values matters: the approved values are what your superintendent builds against.

8. What you acknowledge on each submission

The submission form captures two builder acknowledgments at intake. These appear on the approval letter and bind the construction. They are not boilerplate — they are how we maintain consistent enforcement of the recorded Design Guidelines across the community, which is both a fair-housing requirement and a value-protection mechanism for the homeowners who will own these homes.

  1. Compliance acknowledgment. Builder constructs in accordance with the approved plans, applicable Design Guidelines, and all county and state permits.
  2. Change-control acknowledgment. Any deviation from the approved plans during construction — including substitutions of color, material, masonry coverage, fence type, or elevation — requires written re-approval prior to installation. Material substitution requests typically turn in 24–48 hours through the portal.

Getting started

The portal is live and ready. The fastest path to operating capacity:

  1. Walk the portal once with a coordinator on your team — file a test submission of one plan to see the flow end-to-end.
  2. Submit your full plan library for master approval. We'll work through them on the standard SLA so the library is populated before lot-by-lot construction starts.
  3. Once the library is in place, route lot-by-lot submissions through the portal as construction is scheduled.
