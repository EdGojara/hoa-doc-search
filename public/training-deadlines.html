Meeting Deadlines and Delivering on Your Word

The Foundation Everything Else Is Built On.
The Bedrock Promise

The Bedrock Promise

Our Creed

At Bedrock, we treat every homeowner, board member, vendor, and coworker with dignity and respect. We enforce community standards fairly and consistently. We solve problems, communicate clearly, and continuously improve our systems.

None of that is possible if we don't do what we say we're going to do.

Why This Module Comes Last

Why This Module Comes Last

We saved this for last intentionally.

Look back at the four modules before this one:

  • Hostile homeowners — most started because someone didn't follow up.
  • Demanding board members — most frustrated because management didn't deliver.
  • Compliance resistance — most entrenched because enforcement was inconsistent.
  • Delinquent collections — many escalated because outreach was delayed.

A significant percentage of every difficult interaction you will ever have traces back to one root cause:

Someone said they would do something and didn't.

This module is the foundation everything else is built on.

Foundation

The Most Powerful Thing You Can Do

Do what you said you would do. By when you said you would do it. Every time.

That's it.

It sounds simple.

It is the hardest thing to do consistently in a high-volume, high-interruption business.

And it is the single biggest differentiator between Bedrock and every other HOA management company.

Reality Check

Why This Is So Hard in HOA Management

HOA management is an interruption-driven business.

You plan your day. Three urgent calls come in. A board member needs something immediately. A vendor has an emergency. A homeowner shows up at the office.

By end of day the things you committed to that morning haven't been done.

This is the reality of the job.

The answer is not to commit to less.

The answer is to build systems that make follow-through inevitable.

That's exactly what trustEd is designed to do.

Never make open-ended commitments.

Every "I'll look into it" must become "I'll have an answer for you by [specific day and time]."

Open-ended commitments are where follow-through goes to die.

Standard

The 24-Hour Rule — Revisited

Every communication receives a response within one business day.

Even if you don't have the answer yet.

Even if the issue isn't resolved yet.

"I've received your message and I'm working on it. I'll have an update for you by [specific day]."

That one sentence prevents more hostile calls, demanding board emails, and escalations than almost anything else we do.

Silence is never acceptable.

A brief acknowledgment is always better than nothing.

Service Discipline

The Last 5% — Revisited

Most companies solve problems.

Few companies close loops.

Closing the loop means:

  • Calling when you promised to call
  • Sending the follow-up after the issue is resolved
  • Checking back to confirm the homeowner is satisfied
  • Telling someone "We took care of what you raised"

The last 5% is where trust is actually built.

Anyone can open a ticket.

We close them — and then we confirm they're closed.

Diagnostic

The Four Failure Modes

1
The Forgotten Commitment

You said you'd follow up. You got busy. You forgot.

Prevention · Log every commitment in trustEd the moment you make it. Not later. The moment you make it.
2
The Assumed Handoff

You passed it to someone else and assumed they handled it. They didn't.

Prevention · Ownership doesn't transfer until you confirm the other person has it. Never assume. Always confirm.
3
The Partial Close

You solved part of the problem but not all of it. You considered it done. The homeowner didn't.

Prevention · Before closing any issue, confirm with the homeowner that it's resolved from their perspective.
4
The Silent Miss

You missed a deadline and said nothing. The homeowner found out when they followed up.

Prevention · If you're going to miss a deadline, tell the person before the deadline passes. Not after.
Crisis Protocol

When You're Going to Miss a Deadline

The wrong response: Say nothing and hope they don't notice.

The right response: Tell them before the deadline, reset expectations, and deliver on the new commitment.

"I wanted to reach out before Friday because I told you I'd have an update today and I don't have it yet. Here's where we are and I expect to have a full answer for you by Monday. I'm sorry for the delay and I wanted you to hear from me rather than just not hearing anything."

That single call or email turns a trust-breaking moment into a trust-building one.

People understand delays.

What they cannot forgive is silence.

Discipline

The Commitment Log

Every commitment you make goes into trustEd immediately.

What to log:

  • Who you made the commitment to
  • What you committed to do
  • By when
  • Current status

This is not optional.

This is how we make follow-through systematic instead of dependent on memory.

A commitment that isn't logged is a commitment that's at risk.

Practice

The Daily Habit

Every morning, before you do anything else:

Open your commitment log in trustEd.

  • What is due today?
  • What is overdue?

Address overdue items first.

Schedule due-today items before anything else.

Then handle the day's interruptions.

This habit — five minutes every morning — prevents more problems than any other single practice we can teach you.

Practice

The Weekly Habit

Every Friday before you leave:

Review everything open in your name in trustEd.

  • What needs a follow-up before the weekend?
  • What needs a status update to a homeowner or board member?
  • What can you close?

Never let a homeowner or board member spend the weekend wondering where their issue stands if you can prevent it.

A Friday afternoon update — even just "still working on it, will have more Monday" — goes further than you think.

Discipline

Proactive Communication

The highest form of follow-through isn't responding when asked.

It's communicating before anyone has to ask.

Examples:

  • A vendor is running behind on a repair. You tell the board before they ask.
  • An ARC application is taking longer than expected. You tell the homeowner before the deadline passes.
  • A collections account is approaching attorney referral. You update Ed before he has to check.

Proactive communication is the difference between being managed and being a professional.

Boards that never have to chase us become our strongest advocates.

Boards that constantly have to follow up become our biggest headaches.

The difference is almost always proactive communication.

Language

What To Never Say or Do

Never Say or Do
  • "I'll look into it." (Without a specific follow-up commitment)
  • Letting a deadline pass without notifying the person
  • Assuming someone else handled it without confirming
  • Closing an issue without confirming it's resolved from the other person's perspective
  • Waiting for someone to follow up before you give them a status update
  • "I sent an email." (Without confirming it was received and understood)
Role Play

Scenario 5A — The Forgotten Commitment

Role Play Scenario 5A
The Forgotten Commitment
Setup
A board member calls. You promised them a vendor quote by Wednesday. It's Friday. You forgot.
I'm still waiting on that vendor quote you said you'd have for me Wednesday. What happened?
✗ Wrong Response
"I've been really busy this week. I'll get it to you Monday."
Why it fails
No ownership. No apology. Extends the delay without acknowledging the miss.
✓ Right Response
"You're right and I owe you an apology. I committed to Wednesday and I didn't deliver. That's on me. I have the quote — I can send it to you within the hour. And going forward I'll make sure I reach out to you before a deadline if I'm running behind rather than letting it pass without communication."
Why it works
Took full ownership without excuses. Apologized directly. Fixed it immediately. Committed to better behavior going forward.
Debrief Questions
  • What if you don't actually have the quote yet?
  • How do you rebuild trust with a board member after missing a commitment?
  • What should you have done on Wednesday when you realized you weren't going to make it?
Role Play

Scenario 5B — The Proactive Miss

Role Play Scenario 5B
The Proactive Miss
Setup
You promised a homeowner you'd have their ARC application reviewed by the board within two weeks. It's been ten days and you realize the board meeting is delayed.
✗ The Wrong Approach
Wait until the two-week deadline passes. Then respond when they call.
Why it fails
The trust-breaking moment happens silently. The homeowner finds out by following up. You're already on defense before the conversation starts.
✓ The Right Approach
Call or email before the deadline: "I wanted to reach out before your two-week window closes because I have an update. Our board meeting has been rescheduled and your application will be reviewed at the following meeting on [date] instead. I know that's later than what I told you and I'm sorry for the change. The good news is your application is complete and ready for review. Is there anything you need from me in the meantime?"
Why it works
Got ahead of the miss before it happened. Acknowledged the impact on them. Gave a specific new date. Offered to help in the meantime.
Debrief Questions
  • What if the homeowner is frustrated about the delay?
  • How do you handle a board meeting that keeps getting rescheduled?
  • What can you do to expedite an ARC review when a homeowner has time-sensitive plans?
Role Play

Scenario 5C — The Handoff That Didn't Happen

Role Play Scenario 5C
The Handoff That Didn't Happen
Setup
A homeowner calls angry. They say they were told two weeks ago that someone would call them about their violation. Nobody ever called.
I was told someone would follow up with me about my violation letter. That was two weeks ago. Nobody has called me. What kind of operation are you running?
✗ Wrong Response
"I'm sorry, I don't see any notes about that in your file. Let me find out what happened."
Why it fails
Puts the investigation burden on the homeowner. Sounds like you're doubting them. No ownership.
✓ Right Response
"I completely understand your frustration and I apologize — that follow-up should have happened and it didn't. Regardless of what happened internally, I'm here now and I want to make sure we take care of this today. Can you tell me what the violation was about and what questions you had? I'm going to own this from here."
Why it works
Took immediate ownership without investigating who was at fault first. Apologized for the failure. Moved directly to solving the problem. Made a clear commitment going forward.
Debrief Questions
  • After you resolve the issue with the homeowner, what do you do internally?
  • How do you document a handoff failure in trustEd?
  • What process change would prevent this from happening again?

Reference

The Five Bedrock Values — Applied to Commitments

No. 01
Hospitality
Treat every commitment like a promise to a guest. You wouldn't invite someone to dinner and not tell them you had to cancel. Don't do it to homeowners and board members either.
No. 02
Ownership
The commitment belongs to you until it's done or formally transferred. Not done means not done. No partial credit.
No. 03
Consistency
Follow through every time. Not when it's convenient. Not when you remember. Every time. That's what trust is built on.
No. 04
Communication
If you're going to miss something, say so before the deadline. Not after. Before.
No. 05
Improvement
Every follow-through failure is a system signal. Log it. Learn from it. Fix the process so it doesn't happen again.
