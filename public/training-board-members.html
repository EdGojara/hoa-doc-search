Rude and Demanding Board Members

Serve the Association. Protect the Team.
The Bedrock Promise

The Bedrock Promise

Our Creed

At Bedrock, we treat every homeowner, board member, vendor, and coworker with dignity and respect. We enforce community standards fairly and consistently. We solve problems, communicate clearly, and continuously improve our systems.

This applies to board members too — in both directions.

Foundation

Understanding Who Board Members Are

Most board members are not difficult people.

They are volunteers who care deeply about their community, often with professional backgrounds that make them exacting — former executives, attorneys, engineers, business owners.

Their standards are high because their investment in the community is real.

Understanding that changes how you approach them.

What board members usually want:

  • To be heard and respected
  • Responsive, professional management
  • Clear communication and follow-through
  • A well-run community they can be proud of

What creates friction:

  • Slow responses
  • Lack of follow-through
  • Surprises that could have been anticipated
  • Feeling like management isn't on their side

Most difficult board interactions start with a communication or follow-through failure — not a personality clash.

Principle

The Important Distinction

We serve the board. We also serve the Association.

Those are usually the same thing.

Sometimes they are not.

A board member asking you to selectively enforce a rule, take action outside governing documents, or treat one homeowner differently than another — that is a moment where serving the Association means respectfully pushing back on the board member.

You represent the Association as a whole.

Not one board member.

Not one faction of the board.

The entire Association.

Boundaries

What Crosses the Line

Board members have significant authority.

They do not have unlimited authority.

Reasonable board requests:

  • Updates on community issues
  • Follow-up on vendor performance
  • Enforcement of documented violations
  • Preparation of board meeting materials

Requests that require a careful response:

  • Expecting immediate responses at all hours
  • Directing staff to bypass proper processes
  • Asking management to selectively enforce rules
  • Requesting actions not authorized by governing documents
  • Speaking disrespectfully to staff
When the line is crossed

Your response is not "no."

Your response is "let me find the right path forward."

The Framework

The Respectfully Firm Framework

1
Acknowledge their concern

"I understand this is a priority for you and I want to make sure we handle it correctly."

2
Clarify what's possible

"Let me review the governing documents and provide a recommendation consistent with the Association's policies."

3
Offer a path forward

"Here's what I can do and here's the timeline."

Not: "No."

Not: "Whatever you want."

But: "Here's what I can do and here's why."

That is respectfully firm.

Language

Language That Works

❌ Instead of
"We can't do that."
✓ Say
"Let me check what the governing documents allow and get back to you with options."
❌ Instead of
"That's not how it works."
✓ Say
"The Association's policies require us to handle this through [process]. Here's why that protects both the board and the community."
❌ Instead of
"I'll have to ask Ed."
✓ Say
"I want to make sure I give you the right answer on this. Let me confirm with our team and follow up with you by [specific time]."
❌ Instead of nothing when you miss a deadline
— silence —
✓ Say
"I owe you an update and I apologize for the delay. Here's where we are and here's the new timeline."
Boundaries

When a Board Member Is Disrespectful to Staff

This happens. It is not acceptable.

You have the right to set a boundary calmly and professionally.

"I want to help you work through this. I need us to be able to have this conversation professionally. Can we do that?"

If the behavior continues, document it and escalate to Ed.

Bedrock protects its employees.

Hospitality does not require accepting disrespectful treatment.

Prevention

The Proactive Board Relationship

The best way to handle difficult board members is to prevent the friction before it starts.

Proactive habits:

  • Send updates before they ask for them
  • Flag potential issues early — boards hate surprises
  • Set clear expectations on timelines and follow through
  • When you miss a deadline, acknowledge it immediately and reset expectations
  • Never let a board member wonder what's happening

Boards that feel informed and respected rarely become difficult.

Boards that feel ignored or surprised almost always do.

Standard

The 24-Hour Rule — Board Version

Board members expect faster responses than homeowners.

The Standard

Every board communication receives a response within four business hours during the business day.

Even if the answer is:

"I've received your message and I'm working on it. I'll have an update for you by [specific time]."

Never let a board member send a follow-up asking where you are.

That means you've already broken trust.

Escalation

Attorney Triggers — Board Edition

Escalate immediately to Ed if a board member:

⚠ Standing Caution Notice
Asks for Fair Housing violation
Directs selective enforcement
Contradicts governing documents
Threatens or accuses staff
Asks for legal interpretation
Standing Rule: These are not judgment calls for new employees. Escalate and let Ed handle them.
Role Play

Scenario 2A — The After-Hours Demand

Role Play Scenario 2A
The After-Hours Demand
Setup
A board member texts at 9pm demanding an immediate response to a vendor complaint.
I need you to call the landscaping company right now and tell them their crew left debris all over the entrance. This is unacceptable.
✗ Wrong Response
"I'll handle it first thing in the morning."
Why it fails
Dismissive. No acknowledgment. Doesn't address the urgency they feel.
✓ Right Response
"I completely understand — the entrance is the first thing everyone sees and this reflects on the community. I'll document this tonight and have it addressed first thing tomorrow morning, with a follow-up to you by 10am confirming it's resolved. If this is a safety issue I need to know now so I can escalate immediately."
Why it works
Acknowledged why it matters. Set a clear expectation. Gave a specific follow-up time. Left the door open for genuine emergencies.
Debrief Questions
  • What's the difference between an urgent issue and an emergency?
  • What do you do if the landscaping company doesn't respond?
  • How do you close the loop with the board member the next day?
Role Play

Scenario 2B — The Selective Enforcement Request

Role Play Scenario 2B
The Selective Enforcement Request
Setup
A board member calls asking you to prioritize sending a violation letter to a specific homeowner they have a personal conflict with.
I need you to send a violation notice to 1234 Oak Lane today. They've had issues before and I want it documented.
✗ Wrong Response
"Sure, I'll get that out today."
Why it fails
May be facilitating selective enforcement which creates legal exposure for the Association.
✓ Right Response
"I want to make sure we handle this correctly and protect the Association. Can you help me understand what the current violation is? If there's a documented violation consistent with how we've handled similar situations, I'll absolutely move forward through our standard process. I just want to make sure we're applying the standards consistently across the community."
Why it works
Didn't say no. Didn't comply blindly. Raised the consistency principle professionally. Protected the Association and the board member from their own request.
Debrief Questions
  • What if the board member pushes back and says just do it?
  • When do you escalate to Ed?
  • How do you document this conversation?
Role Play

Scenario 2C — The Disrespectful Board Member

Role Play Scenario 2C
The Disrespectful Board Member
Setup
A board member is rude and dismissive during a phone call, speaking condescendingly.
This is exactly why we need better management. You people never follow through on anything. I don't know why we even pay you.
✗ Wrong Response
Staying silent or becoming defensive.
Why it fails
Either allows the behavior to continue or escalates the conflict.
✓ Right Response
"I hear your frustration and I take it seriously. I want to walk through specifically what fell short so I can make sure it doesn't happen again. Can you help me understand what you were expecting and when?"
If the tone continues: "I want to address your concerns and I'm going to stay on this call. I do need us to be able to have this conversation professionally so we can actually solve the problem. Can we do that?"
Why it works
Took accountability without groveling. Redirected to specifics. Set a boundary calmly. Stayed solution-focused.
Debrief Questions
  • What do you document after this call?
  • When do you report disrespectful treatment to Ed?
  • How do you rebuild a damaged board relationship over time?

Practice

The Weekly Practice

One board scenario per week for the first month.

After each role play:

  • What worked?
  • What would have landed better?
  • What's the one thing to remember?
The One Thing — If you remember nothing else
We serve the board.
We protect the Association.
When those two things align — and they usually do — our job is straightforward.
When they don't align, we respectfully hold the line.
That is what makes Bedrock trustworthy to boards over the long term.
